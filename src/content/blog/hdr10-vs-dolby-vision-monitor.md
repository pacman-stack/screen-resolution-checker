## What Is HDR and Why Should You Care?

HDR stands for High Dynamic Range. It refers to a display's ability to produce a wider range of brightness levels and colors than the standard dynamic range (SDR) content you have been watching for decades. An HDR-capable monitor can show brighter highlights, deeper blacks, and more nuanced color gradations -- all at the same time, in the same frame.

In practical terms, HDR makes sunlight look like actual sunlight, shadow detail stays visible instead of crushing to pure black, and colors pop without looking artificially oversaturated. It is one of the most visually impactful display technologies to emerge in recent years, and it matters whether you are gaming, editing video, or just watching a film.

But HDR is not a single standard. Multiple competing formats exist, and the two most prominent are HDR10 and Dolby Vision. Understanding the difference between them -- and knowing which one your monitor actually supports -- is essential before you invest in HDR content or hardware.

## HDR10: The Open Standard

HDR10 is the baseline HDR format. It was introduced in 2015 by the Consumer Technology Association and is an open, royalty-free standard. That open nature is why it became the most widely adopted HDR format in the industry.

### How HDR10 Works

HDR10 uses **static metadata**. This means the brightness and color information is set once for the entire piece of content -- a single set of instructions that tells your display the maximum brightness, minimum brightness, and color volume for the whole movie, game, or video. The display then maps the HDR content to its own capabilities using that one fixed set of data.

### Key Technical Specs

- **Bit depth:** 10-bit color, which provides 1,024 shades per color channel (compared to 256 shades in 8-bit SDR). This enables over 1 billion color combinations.
- **Color space:** Wide color gamut based on Rec. 2020, though most HDR10 content is mastered in DCI-P3.
- **Peak brightness:** No fixed requirement, but HDR10 content is typically mastered at 1,000 or 4,000 nits.
- **Metadata:** Static -- one set of values per piece of content.
- **Licensing:** Open standard, royalty-free. Any manufacturer can implement it without paying fees.

### Strengths of HDR10

The biggest advantage of HDR10 is ubiquity. Nearly every HDR monitor, TV, streaming service, Blu-ray disc, and game console supports it. If a device says "HDR" without specifying a format, it almost certainly means HDR10. Because it is royalty-free, manufacturers face no barrier to adoption. This makes HDR10 the universal fallback -- even devices that support Dolby Vision also support HDR10.

### Limitations of HDR10

Static metadata is the primary weakness. Because the brightness mapping is set once for the entire content, scenes within the same movie can look suboptimal. A film might have both a dark cave scene and a bright outdoor scene, but the display has to use the same tone mapping for both. This can result in crushed shadows in dark scenes or clipped highlights in bright scenes, depending on how the display handles the static instructions.

## Dolby Vision: The Premium Format

Dolby Vision is a proprietary HDR format developed by Dolby Laboratories. It was designed to address the limitations of HDR10 by introducing dynamic metadata and higher technical ceilings.

### How Dolby Vision Works

Dolby Vision uses **dynamic metadata**, which means the brightness and color mapping instructions can change on a scene-by-scene or even frame-by-frame basis. Instead of one set of instructions for an entire movie, each scene (or each frame) carries its own optimized metadata. The display receives continuous guidance on how to render every moment of the content.

This dynamic approach means a dark interior scene gets tone mapping optimized for shadow detail and low brightness, while the bright exterior scene that follows gets completely different instructions optimized for highlight reproduction. The display adapts in real time.

### Key Technical Specs

- **Bit depth:** Up to 12-bit color, which provides 4,096 shades per color channel and over 68 billion color combinations. In practice, most Dolby Vision content is mastered at 10-bit, but the spec supports 12-bit for future-proofing.
- **Color space:** Rec. 2020 with a wider practical gamut than most HDR10 implementations.
- **Peak brightness:** Supports up to 10,000 nits (theoretical maximum), though current displays top out around 1,000 to 4,000 nits.
- **Metadata:** Dynamic -- per-scene or per-frame adjustment.
- **Licensing:** Proprietary. Manufacturers must pay Dolby a licensing fee to implement Dolby Vision in their devices.

### Strengths of Dolby Vision

Dynamic metadata is the headline feature. It means the content creator (the colorist who grades the film or the developer who masters the game) can optimize how every scene looks on a wide range of displays. A Dolby Vision movie watched on a 600-nit monitor will look different from the same movie on a 1,000-nit monitor, but both will look as good as that display can manage -- because the metadata provides instructions tailored to different brightness capabilities.

The 12-bit color ceiling also gives Dolby Vision a theoretical advantage in color precision, reducing banding in subtle gradients (like a sunset sky fading from orange to deep blue).

### Limitations of Dolby Vision

The licensing requirement is the main barrier. Monitor manufacturers must pay Dolby to include Dolby Vision support, which increases the cost of the display. This is why Dolby Vision is far less common in desktop monitors than in TVs. Most budget and mid-range monitors do not support it.

Additionally, Dolby Vision requires a hardware decoding chip or compatible SoC, which further limits adoption on the PC monitor side. While TVs from LG, Sony, and others widely support Dolby Vision, desktop monitor support remains limited to a small number of high-end models.

## HDR10 vs Dolby Vision: The Full Comparison

Here is a side-by-side breakdown of every meaningful difference between the two formats.

| Feature | HDR10 | Dolby Vision |
|---|---|---|
| **Metadata type** | Static (per-content) | Dynamic (per-scene or per-frame) |
| **Bit depth** | 10-bit | Up to 12-bit |
| **Maximum brightness** | No defined cap (typically mastered at 1,000-4,000 nits) | Up to 10,000 nits (theoretical) |
| **Color space** | Rec. 2020 | Rec. 2020 |
| **Licensing** | Open, royalty-free | Proprietary, requires licensing fee |
| **Monitor adoption** | Nearly universal | Limited to select high-end monitors |
| **TV adoption** | Universal | Widespread on mid-range and premium TVs |
| **Gaming support** | Xbox Series X/S, PS5, PC | Xbox Series X/S (select titles), limited on PC |
| **Streaming support** | Netflix, Disney+, Apple TV+, Amazon Prime | Netflix, Disney+, Apple TV+, Amazon Prime |
| **Content availability** | Very broad | Broad but narrower than HDR10 |
| **Tone mapping quality** | Depends on the display's processor | Guided by content creator's per-scene instructions |
| **Hardware requirement** | Standard HDR-capable hardware | Dolby Vision decoding hardware required |

The takeaway from this table: HDR10 wins on availability and cost. Dolby Vision wins on technical quality and tone mapping precision. For most desktop monitor users, HDR10 is what you will actually use day to day, because Dolby Vision monitor support is still limited.

## HDR10+: The Middle Ground

HDR10+ is Samsung's response to Dolby Vision. It takes the open foundation of HDR10 and adds dynamic metadata on top, aiming to deliver Dolby Vision-like scene-by-scene optimization without the licensing fees.

### How HDR10+ Compares

| Feature | HDR10 | HDR10+ | Dolby Vision |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Metadata** | Static | Dynamic | Dynamic |
| **Bit depth** | 10-bit | 10-bit | Up to 12-bit |
| **Licensing** | Free | Free (certification fee applies) | Paid license |
| **Monitor support** | Very broad | Limited | Limited |
| **TV support** | Universal | Samsung, Panasonic, select others | LG, Sony, Vizio, select others |
| **Content library** | Largest | Growing | Large |

HDR10+ adds dynamic metadata to an open standard, which sounds like the best of both worlds. In practice, its adoption has been slower than expected. The monitor market has largely ignored HDR10+ in favor of either basic HDR10 or, at the premium end, Dolby Vision. On the TV side, HDR10+ is primarily a Samsung ecosystem feature.

For PC monitor buyers, HDR10+ is rarely a decision factor. If your monitor supports it, great -- it is a free upgrade over base HDR10. But you should not choose a monitor based on HDR10+ support alone.

## Monitor HDR Tiers: What DisplayHDR Ratings Actually Mean

Knowing your monitor supports "HDR" is not enough. The quality of HDR implementation varies enormously. VESA created the DisplayHDR certification system to bring some clarity to what HDR actually means on a given monitor.

### DisplayHDR Tiers Explained

| Certification | Peak Brightness | Local Dimming | Black Level | Real-World HDR Quality |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **DisplayHDR 400** | 400 nits | Not required | No requirement | Entry-level -- minimal visible HDR benefit. Brighter than SDR but limited contrast improvement. |
| **DisplayHDR 600** | 600 nits | Required (some form) | 0.1 nits | Noticeable HDR improvement. Highlights have punch, but dark scenes still lack depth on edge-lit panels. |
| **DisplayHDR 1000** | 1,000 nits | Full-array local dimming required | 0.05 nits | Strong HDR performance. Significant contrast between bright highlights and dark shadows. This is where HDR starts looking genuinely impressive. |
| **DisplayHDR 1400** | 1,400 nits | Full-array local dimming required | 0.02 nits | Premium HDR. Near-reference-grade performance for content creation and high-end viewing. |
| **DisplayHDR True Black 400** | 400 nits | Pixel-level (OLED) | 0.0005 nits | OLED-specific. Lower brightness than LCD HDR tiers, but the near-perfect blacks deliver exceptional perceived contrast. |
| **DisplayHDR True Black 600** | 600 nits | Pixel-level (OLED) | 0.0005 nits | Higher-brightness OLED. Excellent HDR with both deep blacks and strong highlights. |

### What This Means for Your Purchase

A DisplayHDR 400 monitor technically supports HDR, but the experience is often underwhelming. The monitor cannot get bright enough to make highlights pop, and without local dimming, the contrast ratio does not improve enough to deliver the "wow factor" that HDR promises.

If you want HDR that actually looks like HDR, **DisplayHDR 600 is the minimum** for a meaningful upgrade over SDR, and **DisplayHDR 1000 is where the experience becomes genuinely impressive**. OLED monitors with True Black certifications offer a different path -- lower peak brightness but perfect per-pixel contrast that makes HDR content look stunning even without hitting 1,000 nits.

The relationship between your monitor's panel quality and its resolution matters here too. A high-resolution panel with poor HDR implementation will not deliver the visual impact of a slightly lower resolution panel with excellent HDR. ## How Resolution and HDR Interact

Resolution and HDR are independent specifications, but they interact in ways that affect your viewing experience.

### Why 4K and HDR Are Usually Paired

Most HDR content -- streaming movies, Blu-ray discs, games -- is mastered at 4K resolution. The HDR grading process happens at 4K because that is the production standard for modern cinema and high-end television. This means you get the most benefit from HDR when you are also watching at 4K, because you are seeing the content exactly as the colorist intended.

That said, HDR works at any resolution. A 1440p HDR monitor will still display HDR content with wider brightness range and better color -- it just downscales the 4K source to fit. The HDR metadata applies to brightness and color, not to resolution.

### Bandwidth Requirements

HDR at higher resolutions demands more bandwidth from your display connection. This is where cable and port standards matter.

| Resolution + HDR | Minimum Connection | Bandwidth Required |
|---|---|---|
| **1080p HDR, 60Hz** | HDMI 2.0 or DisplayPort 1.2 | ~10 Gbps |
| **1440p HDR, 120Hz** | HDMI 2.1 or DisplayPort 1.4 | ~18 Gbps |
| **4K HDR, 60Hz** | HDMI 2.0 (with compression) or DisplayPort 1.4 | ~18 Gbps |
| **4K HDR, 120Hz** | HDMI 2.1 or DisplayPort 2.0 | ~40 Gbps |

If you are running a 4K HDR monitor at 120Hz, you need either HDMI 2.1 or DisplayPort 1.4 with DSC (Display Stream Compression). Older cables and ports will force you to choose between full resolution, full refresh rate, or full HDR color depth -- you cannot have all three simultaneously without sufficient bandwidth.

### GPU Considerations

Your graphics card must support the HDR format and the resolution you want to run. All modern NVIDIA (RTX 30-series and newer) and AMD (RX 6000-series and newer) GPUs support HDR10 output. Dolby Vision support on PC is more limited -- it works through some apps (like Netflix in Edge or the Dolby Access app) but is not universally supported at the OS level in the same way HDR10 is through Windows HD Color.

## How to Check If Your Monitor Supports HDR

Here is how to determine what HDR capabilities your current monitor has.

### On Windows

1. Open **Settings > System > Display**.
2. Click **HDR** (or "Windows HD Color settings" on older builds).
3. Look for "HDR video streaming," "Use HDR," and "Play streaming HDR video." If these toggles exist and are available, your monitor supports HDR10 at minimum.
4. Check the **Display capabilities** section. It will list whether your display supports HDR and at what level.

### On macOS

1. Go to **Apple menu > About This Mac > Displays** (or **System Settings > Displays**).
2. Look for "High Dynamic Range" in the display information. Apple displays (Pro Display XDR, MacBook Pro with Liquid Retina XDR) support HDR natively. External monitors will show HDR capability if supported.

### Check the Monitor's OSD and Spec Sheet

Your monitor's on-screen display (OSD) menu typically has an HDR setting you can enable or disable. Check under display settings or picture mode for options like "HDR Mode," "HDR10," or "Smart HDR." If no HDR option exists in the OSD, your monitor does not support it.

The manufacturer's spec sheet is the most reliable source. Look for:

- **HDR10** -- confirms base HDR support
- **Dolby Vision** -- confirms Dolby Vision support (rare on desktop monitors)
- **DisplayHDR 400/600/1000/1400** -- confirms VESA-certified HDR tier
- **DisplayHDR True Black 400/600** -- confirms OLED HDR certification

### Quick Online Check

Visit [MyScreenResolution.com](/) to identify your display's resolution, pixel ratio, and viewport details. From there, cross-reference your monitor model with its manufacturer spec sheet to confirm HDR tier and format support.

## Which HDR Format Should You Prioritize for a Monitor?

For desktop monitor buyers in 2026, the practical answer is straightforward.

**HDR10 is the format you will use.** It is supported by every HDR monitor, every streaming service, every game console, and every modern GPU. When you enable HDR on your Windows or macOS desktop, you are using HDR10.

**Dolby Vision is a bonus, not a requirement.** If you are buying a premium monitor (particularly a high-end OLED like the LG 32EP950 or ASUS ProArt PA32DC) and it happens to support Dolby Vision, that is a nice extra for watching Dolby Vision content from streaming services. But Dolby Vision should not be a primary purchasing criterion for a desktop monitor, because PC support remains limited and the content you consume on a desktop is overwhelmingly HDR10.

**Focus on the DisplayHDR tier instead.** The difference between a DisplayHDR 400 monitor and a DisplayHDR 1000 monitor is far more impactful than the difference between HDR10 and Dolby Vision on the same panel. A DisplayHDR 1000 monitor running HDR10 will look dramatically better than a DisplayHDR 400 monitor running Dolby Vision, because brightness, contrast, and local dimming matter more than metadata format.

## Conclusion

HDR10 and Dolby Vision both aim to deliver richer, more lifelike images -- but they take different approaches. HDR10 is the universal, open standard with static metadata and 10-bit color. Dolby Vision is the premium, licensed format with dynamic metadata and up to 12-bit color. HDR10+ sits between them, adding dynamic metadata to an open standard but with limited adoption.

For desktop monitor buyers, HDR10 support is effectively guaranteed on any HDR monitor, while Dolby Vision remains confined to a handful of high-end displays. The most important factor in your HDR experience is not which metadata format your monitor supports -- it is the DisplayHDR tier. A monitor with DisplayHDR 1000 or higher, full-array local dimming, and wide color gamut will deliver stunning HDR regardless of whether it runs HDR10 or Dolby Vision. Prioritize the hardware quality of the panel over the format logo on the box.
